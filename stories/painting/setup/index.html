My Painting Setup

Living in Manhattan doesn't allow for a lot of space. I live in a reasonably sized (for Manhattan standards) apartment with my wife and son. My daughter doesn't live with us but she comes down most every day and works out of her old room as a change of pace rather than staying in her apartment and working alone. That leaves us with 4 people sharing space.

There's no room for a real studio as space is at a premium but if you want to paint regularly you don't want to spend 30 minutes doing set up and tear down. I had a french style easel from when I was Bob Ross for Halloween

and it worked well for that with the storage drawer and extending legs but at home, not so much. The telescoping legs that set to a triangle made for a much larger footprint than was necessary. After deciding I was going to stick with this a while, I ended up getting this:

It seems bigger but the footprint is smaller.

I got this branded as a DaVinci easel but there are a bunch out there. I like the shelf on the bottom and I got a couple of hanging baskets to attach to the back to hold paintbrushes which a keep in used tomato cans that I've washed out. :

I throw a cheap plastic tablecloth over the dining table behind the easel and that's where I put my equipment when I work.

I also needed a place to store paintings while they dried. For that I got this rack:

It seems bigger but the footprint is smaller

I got my easel and rack at Jerry's Artorama. I'll write about them in the supplies post. I also got a couple of floor mats and that's pretty much it.

That's it for the setup.

The easel's always out and I try to keep a prepped canvas either on the easel or on my rack so setup is a breeze.

  1. Put the tablecloth over the table

  2. Grab a disposable pallet pad and add paints

  3. Fill my brush cleaning bucket with water (since I'm using water mixable paints) and pull out the trash can / beater rack

and I'm ready to go. Just takes a minute or two.

Cleanup is also pretty quick:

  1. Throw out the paper pallete

  2. Wash the brushes

  3. Remove the tablecloth and throw behind the easel.

Takes about 3 or 4 minutes plus the brush cleaning time.

So that's my setup. Let me know if I should continue this series when I'll talk about my experience with water mixable paints, brushes adn the rest.
